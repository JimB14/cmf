About Challenge My Faith

The Wandering Herd

Are critical thinking and skepticism on the endangered list? Despite access to unparalleled quantities of information, there's a poignant dearth of deliberated conclusions outside academia, especially in social media where it seems that unsupported dogmatism and superficiality proliferate in the brutality of bigoted repetition.

Are we infected by an emotional need to hear the words "I agree?" Does it drive us toward textual applause, likes, smileys and emojies of praise like duty-bound members of a mutual admiration society, or comrades in a battle for beliefs that neither offer or require a reasonable defense or demonstration of comprehension? Social media dialog can be contentiously personal. Alternative perspectives are often interpreted as effrontery, prompting fight or flight responses rather than composed rational debate, suggesting a tacit understanding that posts are designed to elicit support; questions or dissent are unwelcome etiquette violations.

Twitter, in its original text-only format, was the alternative to Facebook's verbosity without boundaries. It introduced character constraints, defining a tweet as a message less than 144 characters in length. To tweet meant capturing everything in a form akin to verbal grunts. The groundwork was laid to take social media superficiality to even greater heights (or depths), and advance its proliferation. Brevity reigned, regardless of the cost to honored traditions of sentence structure, syntax, and the art of literary composition. Verbal grunts replaced rhythm, cadence and the inspiring beauty of communicative prose.

Social media not only created a new breed of writers, it silenced opponents of provincialism who refused to participate in social media prattle. Thoughtful articulation finds no satisfaction in the barren soil of social media's opinionated dogmatism and the unfounded assertions and too many of its faithful users.

How did it come about that the number of followers -- a venerated trifle -- is a measure of worth? Such veneration is an indictment against a culture of spectacle enamored by celebrity. When did we come to value volume over substance? The measure of speech quality is not the number of listeners. Demagogues often draw large crowds. The words "follower" and "friend" are victims of reductionism and redefinition. We imbue blurts with value admirable only by a provincialist with indecorous standards. What have we become? Is what we do in social media truly engaging? Only if we define engagement as mindless validation of undefended conclusions.

The character of superficiality is both its undeliberativeness and its inability to answer the question Why? Why do you believe what you believe? How did you come to believe what you believe instead of something else? What alternatives did you reject, and why? Superficiality cannot delineate or defend a held belief. It tends to believe that repetition is a test of truth: what we hear repeatedly must be true, and what has long been understood to be true is true on the grounds of its longevity. Longevity implies truth and truth implies longevity. Holding a belief superficially means the inability to explain why a belief is a belief. The faith implicit to superficiality is defined succinctly by David Bentley Hart as credulous assent to unfounded premises (Atheist Delusions).

Who understands the difference between an opinion and a position? Who can argue the merits of an opponent's view and then show how their's differs?

When did the sharing one's two cents become an event; an expression of creative intelligence that warrants special names like "post" or "tweet?" When did social media memes become an ipso facto source of reliable intelligence worthy of dissemination as if on a moral mission to conquer untruths, abrogate misconceptions and straighten misinformation? When did comedians become a new kind of philosopher, the reflective sector of society, sorting the true from the false and dispensing distilled and apportioned knowledge? When did entertainment become an educational milieu, or a precursor to learning?

Not long ago the phrase "opinions are a dime a dozen" was a prefixed disclaimer to protect against the charge that one couldn't differentiate opinion from comprehension. To do so was an embarrassment. This habit illumined a belief in the complicated nature of life and a regard for wisdom as a product of the disciplined enterprise of education. Today, in the face of the presentation of a comprehended position, it is not uncommon to hear the dismissive, "Well, that's just your opinion."

So, what is the difference between opinion and position? Simply stated, a position is the product of deliberation; it is the weighing of pros and cons that results in a choice. The holder of a position understands the strengths and weaknesses of a position, and can argue both sides. The holder of an opinion is either ignorant of alternative views or holds a strawman version. Typically, the holder of a position is not threatened by challenges, knowing the pros and cons, whereas the holder of an opinion is often threatened by opposition due to the lack of deliberation. Holders of opinion are unable to the explain why they believe what they believe, how a belief was reached and why. They falsely believe that their opinions are positions. It is the inability to delineate why a belief is held that disinguishes an opinion from a position.

Our culture is a culture of superficiality in search of validation. It says, "Agree with me. Tell me I'm right. Validate my views. Like me. Share me." The tragic character of this dynamic is in what is not sought, namely, truth. What is the nature, its attributes and dynamics, of a culture that produces people who seek validation instead of truth; who want to be "right," but not search for right? Why is it important to hear "You're right" or "I agree with you?" The pusuit of validation is the acceptance of superficiality; it is the opprobrious act of lowering the bar to make the inability to delineate why you believe what you believe, or why you reject what others believe acceptable.
